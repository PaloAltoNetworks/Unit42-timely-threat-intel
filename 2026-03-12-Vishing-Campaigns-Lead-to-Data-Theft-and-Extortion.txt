NOTES:

- Since late December 2025, Unit 42 has responded to numerous incidents across various industries involving voice-based phishing (vishing) that led to data theft and extortion.
- We've observed activity targeting organizations within the Financial Services, Manufacturing, Professional & Legal Services, and Wholesale & Retail sectors
- Based on our observations, most of this activity is likely associated with Bling Libra (aka ShinyHunters) or threat actors affiliated with the broader Scattered LAPSUS$ Hunters alliance.
- In one 2025 case, we saw the threat actors move from access to impact (data exfiltration) in less than 60 minutes.

BACKGROUND:

- We recently released our 2026 edition of the Global Incident Response Report, with several key findings overlapping with this ongoing financially-motivated threat activity.
One of the key findings was that, of the extortion cases we investigated, the usage of encryption dropped from 92% in 2024 to 78% in 2025. It also represented the first year since prior to 2021 where the use of encryption dropped below 89%. 
- Another key finding was that identity weakness played a role in 89% of the cases we responded to in 2025, with 65% of cases involving the use of identity-based initial access techniques. 33% involved a form of social engineering such as vishing.
- The effectiveness of these attacks relies on the gap between identity verification and SaaS-level data permissions.
ANATOMY OF AN ATTACK:
The typical attack lifecycle is characterized by a “low and slow” approach to technical exploitation, instead choosing to weaponize the human element to gain a foothold.
1. Initial Access: Strategic Vishing
The threat actors initiate contact through highly targeted vishing, typically sourcing employee details from professional social networks.
- The “Passkey” Lure: Attackers impersonate internal IT staff, greeting employees by name and citing a mandatory deadline to set up an SSO “passkey.”
- Mobile Redirection: To evade corporate firewalls and URL inspection tools, victims are instructed to use a mobile device to navigate to a visually identical phishing domain.
- STIR/SHAKEN Compliance: Rather than spoofing numbers, which often triggers “Potential Spam” alerts, they use legitimate numbers with fraudulent Caller Name (CNAM) records to appear as “IT Staff.”
2. Persistence via Adversary-in-the-Middle (AiTM)
Once a victim interacts with the phishing site, the threat actors utilize an Adversary-in-the-Middle (AiTM) framework to bypass Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) in real-time.
- Real-Time Relay: As the employee enters credentials, the attacker relays them to the authentic SSO portal using anti-detect browsers and residential proxies to mimic a local login and avoid detection.
- MFA Bypass: When prompted for 2FA, the attacker selected the SSO - Verify TOTP option. The phishing page then mirrored this request, capturing the code from the employee and allowing the attacker to complete the login.
- Ghost Device MFA Enrollment: The attackers used an intentionally sluggish "passkey setup" page to buy time to navigate the victim's actual SSO dashboard and add a new MFA instance — often an Android emulator.
- Obfuscation: The rogue device is often named “Passkey” within the system settings. This ensures that any “New Device Added” notifications are dismissed by the user as a byproduct of their “setup.”
3. Living off the SaaS & Data Exfiltration
The threat actors move laterally not through the network, but through the SaaS dashboard.
- Email Sanitization: Upon gaining access, the first step is often to delete “New Device” or “Login” alerts from the victim's inbox to prevent discovery.
- VPN Hijacking: If internal resources are required, they use the compromised credentials to authenticate through enterprise VPN clients.
- Data Harvesting: The group prioritizes applications with inherent bulk-export capabilities. SaaS platforms like CRMs are a primary target; attackers frequently generate unfiltered “Contacts & Accounts” reports, maximizing field counts to ensure total data exfiltration. With other SaaS platforms, attackers prioritize downloading files with keywords or labeled as “confidential” or “sensitive.”
