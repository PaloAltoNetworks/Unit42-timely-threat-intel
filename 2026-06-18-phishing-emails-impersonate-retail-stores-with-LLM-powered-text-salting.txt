NOTES:

- We found a large email phishing campaign impersonating popular retail stores
- These emails use expiring reward points as a lure
- The emails are likely generated from a shared kit with:
  -- LLM-crafted text
  -- unique nonce padding to evade classifiers
  -- hidden data through CSS tricks

DETAILS:

INFRASTRUCTURE AND DOMAIN ANALYSIS:

- In 98.4% of the emails, phishing links embedded within the message match the email sender’s domain. 
  -- This indicates the threat actor expects to churn through its infrastructure rapidly
  -- Furthermore, almost all the domains were updated in the past 2 months.
- While our dataset contains over 2,000 of these phishing emails, they correspond to 493 unique registered domains. 
- Of these 493 domains, 42.4% were registered in the first half of June 2026.
- An additional 54.2% show WHOIS updated date values in May–June 2026
  -- These updates indicate the threat actor used previously-dormant domains for the campaign.
- 43.8% of the domains use the .com TLD and 11.2% use .garden
- The remaining domains are distributed across 17 other TLDs, including:
  -- .bond
  -- .lat
  -- .living
  -- .skin
- Additionally, at least 67% of the domains follow a dictionary-DGA pattern
  -- These are comprised of short concatenations of two or more English words
- The links embedded in the emails take users to fake retail reward and survey pages.
  -- These pages ultimately redirect to pages that prompt users to enter personal details and payment info.
  -- The pages ask for this info to pay for shipping the "reward" items.

EVASION TACTICS AND OBFUSCATION:

- The visible content is a generic rewards-expiration phish impersonating various retailers
- 88.6% of emails contain at least one hidden text block. They use the following tactics: 
  -- divs with zero width/height + overflow:hidden
  -- font-size:0 / line-height:0
  -- display:none	
  -- opacity:0
  -- visibility:hidden / collapse
  -- clip-path:inset(100%)
  -- clip:rect(0,0,0,0)

OFF-SCREEN ABSOLUTE POSITION:

- Importantly, all emails layer multiple hiding mechanisms for the text blocks.
- Security filters that only remove one or a few specific hiding methods may fail to detect the others.
- The text hidden by the above methods contain blocks of salted text that are likely LLM-generated.
  -- They consist of lengthy text covering benign topics such as recreation, food, books, pets, and travel. 
  -- These narratives are semantically unrelated to the visible phishing lure
  -- They are designed to serve as a classifier-evasion mechanism. 
  -- This technique can be characterized as Bayesian poisoning.
  -- In this type of poisoning, the addition of benign lexical features does the following:
     --- Shifts token distributions
     --- Increases vocabulary diversity
     --- Reduces the relative prominence of phishing-related indicators
- This could degrade the performance of content-based spam and phishing classifiers.
- The hidden text uses common fonts, including:
  -- Trebuchet MS
  -- Helvetica
  -- Georgia/Garamond
  -- Courier New
  -- Gill Sans
  -- Futura/Century Gothic
- They also include special, unique nonces that are short, high-entropy [A-Za-z0-9]{10–15} strings. 
- These are dropped in their own dedicated zero-size divs (seen in 72.7% emails)
- Or they are spliced mid-sentence into the salted prose (seen in 57.0% emails)
- These per-email nonces aim to evade filters that rely on content hashes.
  -- This significantly reduces the effectiveness of fuzzy-hash matching (ssdeep, TLSH)
  -- This is especially effective when combined with the variations in the salted text
- All emails exhibit consistent structural artifacts that are byproducts of a shared templating pipeline
  -- This indicates they were likely generated by a single phishing kit.
